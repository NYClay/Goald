# Personas & Product Vision

## Product Vision — The Emotional Core

**Do NOT make Goald feel like:**
- a bank app
- a spreadsheet
- budgeting software

**Make it feel like:**
> "A cozy game where saving money upgrades your life."

This emotional angle is what makes people stick with Goald. Every UI decision, interaction, and visual element should reinforce this feeling.

---

## Core Vibe

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Cozy | Warm, inviting, low-pressure — not cold or transactional |
| Gamified | Progress bars, visual build stages, milestone rewards |
| Visually rewarding | Each contribution visibly changes the goal image |
| Low-pressure | No punishments, no shame, no alerts for "bad" behavior |
| Progress-focused | Celebrating forward motion, not optimizing budgets |

---

## Audience

Goald's audience is **much broader than "people who budget."**

The strongest users are people who **WANT to save money** but struggle with:
- Consistency (they start and stop)
- Motivation (saving feels slow and invisible)
- Boring finance apps (spreadsheets and graphs don't engage them)

---

## Target Personas

### 1. The Aspiring Dreamer
Someone who has a specific savings goal (travel, home, wedding, big purchase) but has never successfully saved for it before. They need the visual reward loop to stay motivated. Traditional budgeting feels like deprivation — Goald feels like progress.

### 2. The Visual Motivator
A person who responds to progress bars, achievements, and visible outcomes. They lose interest when they can't "see" their money growing toward something tangible. They check the app to watch their goal image build.

### 3. The Consistency-Seeker
Someone who has the income to save but lacks the habit. They've tried other apps but didn't stick with them. They need low-friction check-ins, gentle encouragement, and a reason to come back regularly.

### 4. The Anti-Budgeter
A person who rejects traditional budgeting tools — they find them stressful, restrictive, or judgmental. Goald's framing as a "game" and "progress tool" (not a budget) removes their resistance. They engage because it feels like play, not finance.

### 5. The Gift/Event Saver
Someone saving for a specific occasion (holiday, birthday, anniversary) who doesn't need a full financial planner — just a dedicated, delightful space to set aside money and watch it grow toward that event.

---

## Design Implications

Every feature should be filtered through this question:
> "Does this feel like a cozy game about upgrading your life, or does it feel like finance software?"

- Use encouraging language — never "over budget," "behind schedule," or "penalty"
- Celebrate milestones visibly and joyfully
- Progress is the primary metric — not precision or optimization
- Let people engage on their own terms — no nagging, no rigid schedules
